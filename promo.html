On the dial

Frequencies that really broadcast into the dark

The dial cycles through real number-station callsigns. These aren't invented — they've been logged for decades, reading digit groups to no one who'll admit to listening.

4625 kHz
UVB-76

“The Buzzer.” A monotone drone since the Cold War, broken by the occasional voice.

5448 kHz
S30 · THE PIP

A short repeating pip, day frequency. Russian military, origin unconfirmed.

11545 kHz
E03 · POACHER

“The Lincolnshire Poacher.” Five-figure groups, the fifth digit pitched up.

11530 kHz
HM01 · ATENCIÓN

The Cuban station — spoken digits and digital bursts, still active.

What comes through

A whole number station, built to be listened to

RX · EAR

Navigate by ear

There is no map, and the rooms rebuild themselves the moment you look away. The station is your only compass — learn to read what changes when you turn.

RX · VOICE

A station that sounds real

The whole game is a number station synthesized in WebAudio: many voices, a brown-noise dread that circles the signal without ever swallowing it, and six languages drifting past as you descend.

RX · CRT

A character-mode monitor

One font, one phosphor color, one glyph grid — a text-mode screen bent through a WebGL CRT filter, the signal scrolling as a live spectrogram. Dial the decay up until the picture barely holds.

RX · DEPTH

32 levels of decay

It gets stranger the deeper you go — other tongues, more doors, the walls losing their nerve. Thirty-two levels down, the signal can barely hold itself together.
